Chapter 3: Everyday Life

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key UK public services and how to access them
  • Recognize the structure of the education and healthcare systems
  • Learn about housing, transportation, and financial services
  • Identify important aspects of work and daily life in the UK

1. Healthcare: The National Health Service (NHS)

The National Health Service (NHS) was established in 1948 to provide healthcare for all UK residents, based on need rather than ability to pay.

NHS Structure and Services

  • The NHS provides:
    • Primary care (GPs, dentists, opticians, pharmacists)
    • Secondary care (hospital treatment)
    • Tertiary care (specialized services)
    • Emergency care
    • Mental health services
  • Most NHS services are free at the point of use for UK residents
  • Some services require payment, including:
    • Prescriptions (in England, but free in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland)
    • Dental treatment
    • Eye tests and glasses
  • People on low incomes, pregnant women, children, and those with certain medical conditions may be exempt from these charges

Accessing Healthcare

  • General Practitioners (GPs):
    • Family doctors are the first point of contact for most medical issues
    • You need to register with a local GP practice
    • GPs provide general medical care and can refer patients to specialists
  • NHS 111:
    • Free telephone service for non-emergency medical advice
    • Available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week
  • Emergency Services:
    • For life-threatening emergencies, call 999 or 112
    • Ask for 'Ambulance'
    • Accident and Emergency (A&E) departments at hospitals handle serious injuries and emergencies
  • Walk-in Centers and Minor Injuries Units:
    • Treat minor injuries and illnesses without an appointment
  • Pharmacies:
    • Provide advice on minor health issues
    • Dispense prescribed medicines
    • Sell over-the-counter medications

2. Education System

Education Structure

  • Education is compulsory for all children aged 5-16
  • The academic year runs from September to July
  • School stages:
    Stage Ages Key Information
    Early Years/Pre-school 3-4 Optional but some free provision available
    Primary School 5-11 Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7) and Key Stage 2 (ages 7-11)
    Secondary School 11-16 Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14) and Key Stage 4 (ages 14-16)
    Further Education 16-18 Sixth Form or College (A-levels, BTECs, apprenticeships)
    Higher Education 18+ Universities and colleges (degrees, diplomas)

Types of Schools

  • State Schools:
    • Free to attend and funded by the government
    • Follow the National Curriculum
    • Include community schools, foundation schools, and voluntary schools
  • Academies and Free Schools:
    • Publicly funded but independent from local authority control
    • Have more freedom over curriculum and finances
  • Grammar Schools:
    • State schools that select pupils by ability
    • Admission based on passing the 11+ exam
    • Only in some parts of England
  • Faith Schools:
    • Associated with a particular religion
    • Follow the National Curriculum but may teach religious education according to their faith
  • Independent (Private) Schools:
    • Charge fees and are not funded by the government
    • Do not have to follow the National Curriculum
    • "Public schools" are actually prestigious private schools

Qualifications

  • GCSEs (General Certificate of Secondary Education):
    • Taken at age 16
    • Graded 9-1 (9 being the highest)
  • A-levels (Advanced Levels):
    • Taken at age 18
    • Main university entrance qualification
    • Usually study 3 or 4 subjects
  • Vocational Qualifications:
    • BTECs, NVQs, City & Guilds
    • Focus on practical skills and knowledge for specific jobs
  • Apprenticeships:
    • Combine work, training, and study
    • Available at different levels from intermediate to degree level

3. Housing and Local Services

Types of Housing

  • Owner-occupied: People who own their home, often with a mortgage
  • Private rented: Property rented from a private landlord
  • Social housing: Property rented from a local council or housing association
  • Common housing types:
    • Detached house (not joined to another house)
    • Semi-detached house (joined to one other house)
    • Terraced house (joined to houses on both sides)
    • Flat/Apartment (a self-contained housing unit in a larger building)
    • Bungalow (a one-story house)

Council Tax

  • Paid to local councils to fund local services
  • Based on the value of the property
  • Properties are placed in bands from A (lowest) to H (highest)
  • Discounts available for:
    • Single occupants (25% reduction)
    • Full-time students
    • People on low incomes

Utilities

  • Essential services for homes:
    • Gas and electricity
    • Water and sewerage
    • Telephone and internet
  • Most utility markets are privatized with multiple providers
  • Consumers can choose their gas, electricity, telephone, and internet providers
  • Water services are provided by regional companies

Waste and Recycling

  • Local councils collect household waste and recycling
  • Many areas have separate bins for:
    • General waste
    • Recycling (paper, card, plastic, metal, glass)
    • Garden waste
    • Food waste
  • Large items can be taken to local recycling centers or collected by special arrangement

4. Transportation

Public Transport

  • Trains:
    • Extensive rail network connecting cities and towns
    • Services run by private companies under franchise agreements
    • National Rail provides information on all train services
  • Buses:
    • Operated by private companies, some with local authority subsidies
    • Comprehensive network in cities, less frequent in rural areas
  • London Underground (The Tube):
    • Extensive subway system in London
    • Divided into zones with fares based on distance traveled
  • Trams and Light Rail:
    • Available in some cities including Manchester, Sheffield, Edinburgh, and Nottingham
  • Taxis and Minicabs:
    • Black cabs (hackney carriages) can be hailed on the street
    • Minicabs must be pre-booked
    • Ride-hailing apps like Uber operate in many cities

Driving

  • Drive on the left-hand side of the road
  • To drive you need:
    • A valid driving license
    • Car insurance
    • Vehicle tax
    • MOT certificate for vehicles over 3 years old
  • Foreign licenses:
    • EU/EEA licenses can be used until age 70 or for 3 years after becoming resident (whichever is longer)
    • Other foreign licenses valid for 12 months, then must take a UK test
  • Speed limits:
    • Built-up areas: 30 mph (48 km/h)
    • Single carriageways: 60 mph (96 km/h)
    • Dual carriageways and motorways: 70 mph (112 km/h)

Air Travel

  • Major airports include:
    • London Heathrow (busiest)
    • London Gatwick
    • Manchester
    • Birmingham
    • Edinburgh
    • Glasgow
  • Regional airports serve most parts of the UK

5. Work and Employment

Working Rights

  • Legal minimum working age is 16 (with restrictions)
  • Maximum 48-hour working week (can opt out)
  • National Minimum Wage/National Living Wage varies by age
  • Statutory paid holiday (at least 28 days for full-time workers)
  • Statutory Sick Pay
  • Maternity/Paternity/Shared Parental/Adoption leave
  • Protection from discrimination
  • Right to join a trade union

Finding Work

  • Job sites and recruitment agencies
  • Jobcentre Plus (government employment service)
  • Networking and social media
  • Local and national newspapers

National Insurance

  • A system of contributions that funds:
    • State Pension
    • NHS
    • Unemployment benefits
    • Sickness and disability benefits
  • Everyone with a job receives a National Insurance number
  • Contributions are usually deducted automatically from wages

Income Tax

  • Paid on earnings from employment, self-employment, pensions, and some benefits
  • Personal Allowance: amount earned before paying tax
  • Tax bands:
    • Basic rate: 20%
    • Higher rate: 40%
    • Additional rate: 45%
  • Usually collected through PAYE (Pay As You Earn) for employees
  • Self-employed people submit an annual Self Assessment tax return

6. Money and Financial Services

Currency

  • Pound sterling (£) is the official currency
  • £1 = 100 pence (p)
  • Coins: 1p, 2p, 5p, 10p, 20p, 50p, £1, £2
  • Notes: £5, £10, £20, £50
  • Scotland and Northern Ireland issue their own banknotes (legal tender throughout UK)

Banking

  • Main types of financial institutions:
    • Banks
    • Building societies
    • Credit unions
  • Common account types:
    • Current accounts for everyday banking
    • Savings accounts
    • ISAs (Individual Savings Accounts) - tax-free savings
  • Payment methods:
    • Debit cards
    • Credit cards
    • Contactless payments
    • Mobile banking and payment apps
    • Direct debits and standing orders
    • Cheques (less common now)
  • Basic bank accounts available for those with poor credit history

Opening a Bank Account

You'll typically need:

  • Proof of identity (passport, driving license)
  • Proof of address (utility bill, council tax bill)
  • Proof of immigration status for non-UK citizens

Credit and Debt

  • Types of borrowing:
    • Mortgages (home loans)
    • Personal loans
    • Credit cards
    • Overdrafts
  • Credit reference agencies maintain credit history records
  • Free debt advice available from charities like Citizens Advice and StepChange

7. Communications and Media

Television

  • TV license required to watch or record live TV or use BBC iPlayer
  • Public service broadcasters:
    • BBC (British Broadcasting Corporation) - funded by TV license fee
    • Channel 4 - publicly owned but commercially funded
    • ITV and Channel 5 - private companies with public service obligations
  • Pay TV providers include Sky, Virgin Media, and BT
  • Streaming services like Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Disney+ are popular

Radio

  • BBC national radio stations (Radio 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 Live, 6 Music, etc.)
  • Commercial radio networks
  • Local and community radio stations
  • Digital radio (DAB) available across most of the UK

Newspapers

  • National daily newspapers:
    • Broadsheets/quality press: The Guardian, The Times, The Daily Telegraph, Financial Times
    • Tabloids: The Sun, Daily Mail, Daily Mirror, Daily Express
  • Sunday editions often have different names
  • Local and regional newspapers
  • Free papers in major cities

Internet and Telecommunications

  • Multiple providers for broadband and mobile services
  • High-speed broadband available in most urban areas
  • Mobile network coverage is generally good across populated areas
  • UK landline telephone code is +44
  • For emergency services call 999 or 112

Post

  • Royal Mail provides the national postal service
  • Post Office branches provide:
    • Postal services
    • Banking services
    • Bill payments
    • Foreign currency exchange
    • Passport and driving license applications
  • UK postcodes have two parts: an outward code (area and district) and inward code (sector and unit), e.g., EC1A 1BB

8. Benefits and Social Security

Welfare System

  • Main welfare support:
    • Universal Credit: Combined benefit for working-age people
    • Personal Independence Payment (PIP): For long-term health conditions or disabilities
    • State Pension: Regular payments once you reach State Pension age
    • Pension Credit: Extra money for pensioners on low incomes
    • Child Benefit: Money to help with raising children
    • Carer's Allowance: For people caring for someone with substantial needs
  • Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) administers most benefits
  • HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) handles tax credits and Child Benefit

Accessing Benefits

  • Apply online for most benefits
  • Jobcentre Plus provides face-to-face support
  • Citizens Advice offers free, independent advice
