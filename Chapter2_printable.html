Chapter 2: British Values and Principles

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the fundamental British values
  • Comprehend the UK's democratic system and key institutions
  • Recognize the relationship between the government, monarchy, and people
  • Identify the role of devolved administrations

1. Fundamental British Values

The UK is founded on fundamental values and principles which are:

  • Democracy
  • The rule of law
  • Individual liberty
  • Tolerance and respect for different faiths and beliefs
  • Participation in community life

Democracy

  • Citizens have a voice in how the country is governed through free and fair elections
  • People can join political parties, campaign for political positions, and vote in elections
  • Parliament is at the heart of the UK's democracy
  • UK citizens aged 18 or over can vote (unless they are in prison or have been found guilty of certain electoral offenses)
  • Elections use a "first past the post" system where the candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins

The Rule of Law

  • No one is above the law - the same laws apply to everyone
  • The law is enforced equally and fairly
  • The legal system is independent of the government
  • Citizens are presumed innocent until proven guilty
  • Everyone has the right to a fair trial

Individual Liberty

  • Freedom to act and express yourself as you wish, as long as you do not break the law or infringe on others' rights
  • Freedom of speech and expression
  • Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
  • Freedom of association (to form and join clubs, societies, and unions)

Respect and Tolerance

  • The UK is a diverse society with many different ethnic, religious, and cultural communities
  • Mutual respect is expected regardless of differences in race, gender, sexuality, religion, or political views
  • Discrimination on grounds of race, religion, gender, disability, or sexuality is illegal
  • All communities are expected to live by the rule of law

2. British Democracy

Constitutional Monarchy

  • The UK is a constitutional monarchy where the monarch is the Head of State but has limited powers
  • The monarch's role is largely ceremonial and symbolic
  • The monarch must remain politically neutral
  • The current monarch is King Charles III
  • Key ceremonial roles:
    • Opening Parliament each year
    • Approving new laws (Royal Assent)
    • Receiving foreign heads of state
    • Representing the UK abroad

Parliamentary Democracy

  • Parliament is the supreme legal authority in the UK
  • Parliament consists of:
    • The Sovereign (King or Queen)
    • The House of Lords
    • The House of Commons
  • Parliament has three main functions:
    • Making and changing laws
    • Examining government policy and administration
    • Debating current issues

The House of Commons

  • The main chamber of Parliament
  • 650 elected Members of Parliament (MPs) representing geographical areas called constituencies
  • Led by the Speaker, who chairs debates
  • The political party with the most MPs usually forms the government
  • General elections must be held at least every 5 years

The House of Lords

  • The second chamber of Parliament
  • Members are not elected but appointed by the monarch on the advice of the Prime Minister
  • Some Lords are hereditary peers and some are bishops of the Church of England
  • The House of Lords can:
    • Amend or reject bills from the House of Commons
    • Delay legislation, but not block it permanently
    • Ask the government to think again about proposals

The Prime Minister and Cabinet

  • The Prime Minister is the head of the UK government
  • Usually the leader of the party with the most MPs in the House of Commons
  • Responsible for:
    • Appointing ministers
    • Leading the Cabinet
    • Being the principal government spokesperson
    • Making major decisions and coordinating government policy
  • The Cabinet is made up of about 20 senior ministers (Secretaries of State) who run government departments
  • The Prime Minister lives and works at 10 Downing Street

The Official Opposition

  • The second-largest party in the House of Commons
  • Forms a "Shadow Cabinet" that scrutinizes government ministers
  • The leader is called the Leader of the Opposition
  • Key role is to challenge the government and hold them to account

Example: How a Bill Becomes Law

  1. Bill introduced in either House of Commons or House of Lords
  2. Second reading with debate on main principles
  3. Committee stage for detailed examination
  4. Report stage for consideration of amendments
  5. Third reading for final debate
  6. Process repeats in the other House
  7. Both Houses agree on final text
  8. Royal Assent from the monarch
  9. Bill becomes an Act of Parliament (law)

The Civil Service

  • Politically neutral staff who run government departments
  • Civil servants implement the policy decisions made by ministers
  • They remain in place regardless of which party is in power
  • They provide impartial advice to ministers

3. Devolved Administrations

Since 1997, some powers have been transferred from the UK Parliament to bodies in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. This process is known as devolution.

Scottish Parliament

  • Established in 1999
  • Based in Edinburgh
  • 129 Members of the Scottish Parliament (MSPs)
  • Led by the First Minister of Scotland
  • Powers include:
    • Health and social services
    • Education and training
    • Local government
    • Housing
    • Justice and policing
    • Agriculture, forestry, and fishing
    • Environment
    • Tourism, sport, and heritage
    • Economic development and internal transport

Welsh Parliament (Senedd Cymru)

  • Established in 1999 (formerly called the National Assembly for Wales)
  • Based in Cardiff
  • 60 Members of the Senedd (MSs)
  • Led by the First Minister of Wales
  • Powers include:
    • Health and social welfare
    • Education and training
    • Economic development
    • Housing
    • Transport
    • Planning
    • Environment
    • Agriculture
    • Culture and Welsh language

Northern Ireland Assembly

  • Established under the Good Friday Agreement (Belfast Agreement) of 1998
  • Based at Stormont in Belfast
  • 90 Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs)
  • Led by a First Minister and deputy First Minister
  • Powers include:
    • Health and social services
    • Education
    • Agriculture
    • Social security
    • Housing
    • Economic development
    • Environment
    • Transport
  • Has experienced periods of suspension when power-sharing arrangements broke down

Reserved Matters

Some powers remain "reserved" to the UK Parliament at Westminster:

  • Constitutional matters
  • Foreign policy
  • Defense
  • National security
  • Immigration and nationality
  • Nuclear energy
  • UK-wide taxation
  • Employment legislation
  • Social security (with some exceptions)
  • Trade and industry
  • Most transport safety and regulation

4. The Legal System

There are three separate legal systems in the UK:

  • England and Wales share one system
  • Scotland has its own legal system
  • Northern Ireland has its own legal system

The Judiciary

  • Judges are independent of the government
  • They interpret and apply the law
  • Senior judges are appointed by the monarch on the advice of the Prime Minister
  • The principle of "judicial independence" means judges must be free from political influence

English and Welsh Courts

  • Magistrates' Courts: Handle minor criminal cases, some family matters, and can send more serious cases to the Crown Court
  • Crown Court: Deals with serious criminal cases, appeals from Magistrates' Courts, and cases sent for trial by Magistrates
  • County Courts: Handle most civil cases such as debt, property disputes, and family matters
  • High Court: Deals with complex civil cases and appeals from County Courts
  • Court of Appeal: Handles appeals from the Crown Court and High Court
  • Supreme Court: The final court of appeal for all UK civil cases and English, Welsh, and Northern Irish criminal cases

Scottish Courts

  • Sheriff Courts: Handle most criminal and civil cases
  • High Court of Justiciary: Deals with serious criminal cases and criminal appeals
  • Court of Session: Scotland's highest civil court
  • Appeals from the Court of Session go to the UK Supreme Court

Northern Ireland Courts

  • Similar structure to England and Wales
  • Magistrates' Courts and Crown Court for criminal cases
  • County Courts and High Court for civil cases
  • Court of Appeal of Northern Ireland
  • Appeals go to the UK Supreme Court

The Police

  • Independent of the government
  • There are 45 police forces across the UK
  • Police officers work to:
    • Protect life and property
    • Maintain order
    • Prevent and detect crime
    • Bring offenders to justice
  • Special police forces include:
    • British Transport Police
    • Ministry of Defence Police
    • Civil Nuclear Constabulary

5. Rights and Responsibilities

Human Rights

  • The Human Rights Act 1998 incorporated the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law
  • Key rights include:
    • Right to life
    • Prohibition of torture
    • Right to liberty and security
    • Right to a fair trial
    • Respect for private and family life
    • Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
    • Freedom of expression
    • Freedom of assembly and association
    • Right to education
    • Prohibition of discrimination

Citizen Responsibilities

  • Respecting the rights of others
  • Obeying the law
  • Serving on a jury if called
  • Voting in elections (although this is not compulsory)
  • Paying taxes
  • Helping others in the community
  • Protecting the environment

6. Participation in Democracy

Voting

  • UK citizens aged 18 or over can vote (16 for some Scottish elections)
  • Irish and qualifying Commonwealth citizens living in the UK can also vote
  • Must be on the electoral register to vote
  • Types of elections:
    • UK Parliamentary (general) elections
    • Local government elections
    • Elections to devolved legislatures
    • Police and Crime Commissioner elections (England and Wales)
    • Mayoral elections in some areas

Other Ways to Participate

  • Joining a political party
  • Becoming a local councillor
  • Contacting your MP
  • Joining a campaigning organization
  • Volunteering in the community
  • Signing petitions (including official e-petitions)
  • Participating in peaceful demonstrations

Key Concepts to Remember

Concept Description
Parliamentary sovereignty Parliament is the supreme legal authority and can create or end any law
Constitutional monarchy A system where the monarch is the head of state but their powers are limited by the constitution
Rule of law Everyone is subject to the same laws, which are publicly created and fairly enforced
Devolution The transfer of powers from central government to regional administrations
Separation of powers The division of government responsibilities between the executive, legislature, and judiciary
Civil liberties The basic rights and freedoms guaranteed to individuals
First past the post Electoral system where the candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins
Royal Assent Formal approval by the monarch for a bill to become law
