Chapter 6: Government and Law

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the UK's constitutional arrangements
  • Recognize the structure and function of Parliament
  • Identify how laws are made and enforced
  • Learn about the electoral system and voting process
  • Understand the legal system and courts
  • Recognize the requirements for citizenship

1. Constitutional Arrangements

The UK does not have a single written constitution. Instead, it has a collection of laws, conventions, and court judgments that together establish how the country is governed.

Key Constitutional Principles

  • Parliamentary sovereignty: Parliament is the supreme legal authority and can create or end any law
  • Rule of law: Everyone is subject to the same laws, which are publicly created and fairly enforced
  • Constitutional monarchy: The monarch is the Head of State but has limited powers
  • Separation of powers: Division between legislature (Parliament), executive (Government), and judiciary (Courts)

Sources of the Constitution

  • Legislation: Acts of Parliament, such as:
    • Bill of Rights 1689
    • Act of Settlement 1701
    • Acts of Union 1707 (England and Scotland)
    • Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949
    • Human Rights Act 1998
    • Constitutional Reform Act 2005
    • Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011
  • Conventions: Non-legal rules and customs that are followed by political institutions
  • Common law: Legal principles developed through court judgments
  • Works of authority: Academic writings that are considered influential

2. The Monarchy

Role of the Monarch

  • Head of State of the United Kingdom
  • Largely ceremonial role with limited practical power
  • Must remain politically neutral
  • Acts on the advice of ministers
  • Current monarch: King Charles III

Key Functions of the Monarch

  • Constitutional duties:
    • Appointing the Prime Minister (traditionally the leader of the majority party)
    • Opening Parliament with the King's/Queen's Speech
    • Giving Royal Assent to bills (formal approval for a bill to become law)
    • Appointing judges, ministers, and other officials on advice of the government
  • Representational duties:
    • Representing the UK abroad on state visits
    • Receiving foreign heads of state
    • Presenting honors and awards
  • Head of the Commonwealth:
    • Symbolic leader of the Commonwealth of Nations
    • Attends Commonwealth Heads of Government meetings

Succession to the Throne

  • Hereditary monarchy
  • Succession determined by descent and parliamentary statute
  • Since 2013, male heirs no longer take precedence over female heirs
  • The heir to the throne is Prince William (Prince of Wales)

3. Parliament

Parliament is the supreme legislative body in the UK. It consists of the Sovereign (King or Queen), the House of Lords, and the House of Commons.

Functions of Parliament

  • Making and changing laws
  • Examining and challenging the work of the government (scrutiny)
  • Debating important issues
  • Checking and approving government spending (budget)

House of Commons

  • 650 elected Members of Parliament (MPs) representing geographical areas called constituencies
  • Lower chamber but more powerful than the House of Lords
  • MPs are elected at general elections or by-elections
  • Led by the Speaker who chairs debates
  • The government is formed by the party that can command a majority in the House of Commons
  • Main functions:
    • Initiating and passing legislation
    • Controlling taxation and government spending
    • Scrutinizing government policies through questions and debates
    • Holding ministers accountable

House of Lords

  • Upper chamber with around 800 members
  • Members not elected but appointed by the monarch on advice of the Prime Minister
  • Types of peers:
    • Life peers (majority, appointed for life)
    • Hereditary peers (92 remain after House of Lords Act 1999)
    • Bishops of the Church of England (26 Lords Spiritual)
  • Functions:
    • Reviewing and revising legislation from the Commons
    • Delaying bills (excluding money bills) for up to one year
    • Acting as the final court of appeal (until 2009 when Supreme Court was established)
    • Scrutinizing government through questions and debates

How a Bill Becomes Law

The Legislative Process

  1. First Reading: Formal introduction of the bill, no debate
  2. Second Reading: General debate on principles of the bill
  3. Committee Stage: Detailed examination, amendments may be made
  4. Report Stage: House considers changes made in committee
  5. Third Reading: Final debate, further amendments may be possible
  6. The bill then goes to the other House and goes through the same stages
  7. If the two Houses disagree, the bill may go back and forth ("ping pong")
  8. Royal Assent: Monarch formally approves the bill, which becomes an Act of Parliament

Parliamentary Sovereignty

  • Parliament can make, amend, or repeal any law
  • No Parliament can bind a future Parliament
  • Laws made by Parliament cannot be overturned by the courts
  • This principle has been modified somewhat by:
    • Membership of the European Union (pre-Brexit)
    • Devolution to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland
    • Human Rights Act 1998

4. Government

The Executive Branch

  • Prime Minister:
    • Head of Government
    • Usually the leader of the party with the most MPs in the Commons
    • Appointed by the monarch
    • Lives and works at 10 Downing Street
    • Responsible for appointing and dismissing ministers
    • Chairs the Cabinet
  • Cabinet:
    • Group of about 20-25 senior ministers
    • Collectively responsible for government policy
    • Each member typically heads a government department
    • Key positions include Chancellor of the Exchequer, Foreign Secretary, Home Secretary
  • Ministers:
    • Responsible for specific areas of policy
    • Lead government departments
    • Answer to Parliament for their actions and decisions

The Civil Service

  • Politically neutral staff who work for the government
  • Remain in place when governments change
  • Implement policies decided by ministers
  • Provide impartial advice to ministers
  • Headed by the Cabinet Secretary (most senior civil servant)

The Opposition

  • The second-largest party in the House of Commons
  • Led by the Leader of the Opposition
  • Forms a Shadow Cabinet to scrutinize government ministers
  • Proposes alternative policies
  • Key role in holding the government to account

5. Elections and Voting

General Elections

  • Elections for the House of Commons
  • Held at least every 5 years
  • 650 constituencies each elect one MP
  • First-past-the-post system: candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins
  • Prime Minister can request the monarch to dissolve Parliament and call an election before the 5-year term ends

Eligibility to Vote

  • British, Irish, and qualifying Commonwealth citizens aged 18 or over
  • Must be registered to vote
  • Must be resident at a UK address (or a UK citizen living abroad who has been registered to vote in the UK in the last 15 years)
  • Not legally excluded from voting (e.g., serving prisoners, House of Lords members)

Other Elections

  • Local elections:
    • For local councils
    • Different electoral systems in different parts of the UK
  • Devolved administration elections:
    • Scottish Parliament
    • Welsh Parliament (Senedd)
    • Northern Ireland Assembly
    • Use proportional representation systems
  • Mayoral elections:
    • For directly elected mayors in some cities and regions
  • Police and Crime Commissioner elections:
    • For commissioners who oversee police forces in England and Wales

6. The Legal System

The UK has three separate legal systems: one for England and Wales, one for Scotland, and one for Northern Ireland. They share many common features but have important differences.

Key Principles

  • Rule of law: Everyone is subject to the same laws
  • Judicial independence: Judges are independent of the government
  • Due process: Fair procedures in legal proceedings
  • Presumption of innocence: Innocent until proven guilty
  • Right to a fair trial: Everyone has the right to a fair and public hearing

Types of Law

  • Criminal law:
    • Deals with crimes against the state or society
    • Prosecuted by the Crown Prosecution Service in England and Wales
    • Burden of proof is "beyond reasonable doubt"
  • Civil law:
    • Deals with disputes between individuals or organizations
    • Cases brought by individuals or organizations
    • Burden of proof is "on the balance of probabilities"
    • Includes contract, tort, property, family law, etc.

Courts in England and Wales

Court Function
Magistrates' Courts Handle minor criminal cases, some civil matters, and send serious cases to Crown Court
Crown Court Handles serious criminal cases, appeals from Magistrates' Courts, and cases sent for trial
County Courts Handle most civil cases such as debt problems, personal injury claims, and family matters
High Court Handles complex civil cases and appeals from County Courts and Magistrates' Courts
Court of Appeal Handles appeals from the Crown Court and High Court
Supreme Court Final court of appeal for all UK civil cases and English, Welsh, and Northern Irish criminal cases

Scottish Legal System

  • Based on both civil law (Roman law) and common law traditions
  • Key courts:
    • Justice of the Peace Courts: Minor criminal offenses
    • Sheriff Courts: Most criminal and civil cases
    • High Court of Justiciary: Serious criminal cases and criminal appeals
    • Court of Session: Highest civil court
    • UK Supreme Court: Final court of appeal for civil cases
  • Three possible verdicts in criminal cases: guilty, not guilty, or not proven

Northern Ireland Legal System

  • Similar to England and Wales but with some differences
  • Key courts:
    • Magistrates' Courts
    • County Courts
    • Crown Court
    • High Court
    • Court of Appeal
    • UK Supreme Court

The Judiciary

  • Independent of the government
  • Judges appointed on merit, not elected
  • Senior judges appointed by the monarch on advice of the Prime Minister
  • Judicial Appointments Commission recommends candidates
  • Cannot be removed from office except in exceptional circumstances

7. Law Enforcement

The Police

  • There are 45 police forces across the UK
  • Each force headed by a Chief Constable (or Commissioner in London)
  • Police are operationally independent of government
  • Main roles:
    • Preventing and detecting crime
    • Maintaining public order
    • Protecting people and property
    • Traffic control
  • Special police forces include:
    • British Transport Police
    • Ministry of Defence Police
    • Civil Nuclear Constabulary
  • England and Wales forces overseen by elected Police and Crime Commissioners
  • Scotland has a single Police Scotland force
  • Northern Ireland has the Police Service of Northern Ireland (PSNI)

Criminal Justice Process

  1. Investigation: Police gather evidence about a crime
  2. Charge: Police or Crown Prosecution Service decide whether to charge a suspect
  3. Court appearance: First hearing usually at Magistrates' Court
  4. Trial: At Magistrates' Court or Crown Court depending on seriousness
  5. Sentencing: If found guilty, punishment is determined
  6. Appeal: Possible to appeal against conviction or sentence

8. Human Rights and Equality

Human Rights Act 1998

  • Incorporates the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law
  • Allows people to enforce Convention rights in UK courts
  • Key rights protected:
    • Right to life
    • Prohibition of torture
    • Right to liberty and security
    • Right to a fair trial
    • Respect for private and family life
    • Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
    • Freedom of expression
    • Freedom of assembly and association
    • Right to education
    • Prohibition of discrimination

Equality Legislation

  • Equality Act 2010:
    • Consolidates previous anti-discrimination laws
    • Prohibits discrimination based on "protected characteristics"
    • Protected characteristics include age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation
    • Applies to employment, education, provision of goods and services, and public functions
  • Equality and Human Rights Commission:
    • Independent body that monitors and enforces equality and non-discrimination laws
    • Provides guidance and campaigns to promote equality

9. UK Citizenship

Routes to Citizenship

  • Birth:
    • Born in the UK to a parent who is a British citizen or settled in the UK
    • Born in the UK after 1983 and lived there until age 10
  • Descent:
    • Born outside the UK to a parent who is a British citizen
  • Naturalization:
    • Adults who have lived legally in the UK for at least 5 years (3 years if married to a British citizen)
  • Registration:
    • For certain people with connections to the UK
    • Including children born in the UK who are not automatically citizens

Requirements for Naturalization

  • Residence:
    • 5 years' legal residence (3 years if married to a British citizen)
    • No more than 450 days absent during the 5 years (270 days if 3 years)
    • No more than 90 days absent in the final 12 months
  • Good character:
    • No serious criminal record
    • No recent breaches of immigration rules
  • Knowledge of English, Welsh, or Scottish Gaelic:
    • Must meet at least B1 CEFR level (intermediate)
    • Usually demonstrated through a certified test
  • Knowledge of Life in the UK:
    • Pass the Life in the UK test
    • Multiple-choice test on British traditions, customs, history, and government
  • Citizenship ceremony:
    • Attend a ceremony where you make an oath or affirmation of allegiance to the monarch
    • Make a pledge to respect the rights, freedoms, and laws of the UK

The Citizenship Pledge

"I will give my loyalty to the United Kingdom and respect its rights and freedoms. I will uphold its democratic values. I will observe its laws faithfully and fulfill my duties and obligations as a British citizen."

Key Government and Law Concepts to Remember

Concept Description
Parliamentary sovereignty Parliament is the supreme legal authority and can create or repeal any law
Constitutional monarchy The monarch is head of state but has limited powers and acts on advice of ministers
Cabinet government Executive decisions are made collectively by senior ministers
Royal Assent Formal approval of bills by the monarch before they become law
First-past-the-post Electoral system where the candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins
Rule of law Everyone is equal before the law, which is applied fairly and consistently
Judicial independence Judges make decisions free from political influence
Devolution Transfer of powers from central government to administrations in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland
